Game Changers · Gaming · May 2023

Skill for the Kill

StateSpace is building the gym, the coach, and the scouting combine for esports — all in one platform.

"Game Changer." Two words used to describe the people who dare to challenge the status quo — who bet on ideas so daring yet intuitive that you wonder, in hindsight, why things weren't done this way all along.

I'll admit a personal bias here: I've been obsessed with esports for years. So when I came across StateSpace on TechCrunch and Crunchbase, it hit differently. This is a company that understood something the industry had been ignoring — competitive gamers need structured skill development, not just more hours logged.

What StateSpace does

StateSpace is an esports venture based out of New York. Their flagship product, Aim Lab, is a training platform for first-person shooter gamers. Think of it as a gym for your aim — a controlled environment where players can practice the precise mechanics that matter in competitive games like Valorant, CS:GO, or Apex Legends.

But Aim Lab isn't just a shooting range. It's diagnostic. The platform analyses a player's performance, identifies specific weaknesses — tracking, flicking, target switching — and prescribes targeted drills to address them. It mirrors the environment of the games players actually play, so the skill transfer is immediate and measurable.

What Aim Lab gives you
Diagnosis: pinpoints exactly where your aim breaks down
Training: game-specific drills in a controlled environment
Progress tracking: measurable improvement over time, not just vibes

Why this is a genuine game-changer

The esports industry has a measurement problem. Current matchmaking systems rely on blunt instruments — kill-death ratio, matches played, win rate. These metrics capture outcomes, not skill. They tell you what happened, not why. And they're easy to game.

StateSpace is building toward something more ambitious: a standardised metric for gamer skill, analogous to the NFL Scouting Combine. The Combine gives teams a common language for evaluating athletes across positions, schools, and playing styles. No such standard exists in esports. If StateSpace can establish one, they won't just have a training app — they'll own the infrastructure for how talent is identified, developed, and valued across the entire competitive gaming ecosystem.

"What gets measured gets managed."

— Peter Drucker
Esports has never had a rigorous answer to "how good is this player, really?" StateSpace is building the answer. That's a platform play, not just a product.

If the standardised metric succeeds, the knock-on effects are significant: better matchmaking means better player experiences. Better player experiences means more engagement. More engagement means a healthier competitive ecosystem. And a healthier ecosystem means more money flowing into the industry — from teams, sponsors, and broadcasters who can now make data-driven decisions about talent.

The concerns worth taking seriously

The most obvious threat: the game developers themselves. Epic Games (Fortnite), Valve (CS:GO), and Riot Games (Valorant) could each build native training modes directly into their games. They have the player data, the engineering resources, and — crucially — the distribution. If Valve decides to build Aim Lab into CS:GO, StateSpace loses its largest user base overnight.

StateSpace's defence is neutrality. A training platform that works across all games is more valuable than any single game's native mode. The Scouting Combine works because it's independent — teams trust the data precisely because it wasn't collected by another team. If StateSpace can position itself as the neutral, cross-game standard, the game developers become partners rather than competitors.

Whether they can get there before a developer decides to move is the central tension in the business. Timing matters enormously here.

What excites me most about StateSpace isn't the training product — it's the long game. Building a universal skill standard for esports would be genuinely infrastructural. The kind of thing that, once established, becomes the default. That's a rare opportunity. And in a space I love watching, it's the kind of bet I'd love to see pay off.
