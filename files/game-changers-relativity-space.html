Game Changers · Deep Tech · May 2023

Infinity and Beyond

How Relativity Space is using 3D printing, AI, and audacity to reinvent rocket manufacturing from the ground up.

"Game Changer." Two words used to describe the people who dare to challenge the status quo — who bet on ideas so daring yet intuitive that you wonder, in hindsight, why things weren't done this way all along.

Today's company is one I've been watching closely. It operates in a domain where failure is literal and the stakes are as high as the sky they're trying to reach: aerospace. And the company is Relativity Space.

What Relativity Space does

Relativity Space is a private-launch space venture based out of Inglewood, California, founded by Tim Ellis and Jordan Noone. Its mission is simple to state and extraordinarily difficult to execute: build rockets faster, cheaper, and smarter than anyone has before.

Their answer is to rethink the manufacturing process entirely. Traditional rocket manufacturing is slow, labour-intensive, and relies on thousands of individually machined parts assembled by hand. Relativity's approach: print the whole thing. Using their proprietary metal 3D printing technology — combined with AI and robotics — they've compressed the manufacturing timeline by a factor of 10x, while simultaneously digitising the entire process.

The numbers
Traditional rocket manufacturing: thousands of parts, years of assembly
Relativity's Terran 1: ~85% 3D printed — the most of any rocket ever built
Manufacturing time reduction: 10x faster than conventional methods

Why this is a genuine game-changer

Rocket manufacturing has three chronic problems that have plagued the industry since its inception: speed, quality, and cost. Relativity's approach takes all three head-on at once, which is rare — most engineering solutions trade one off against another.

Speed matters because the faster you can manufacture, the faster you can iterate, test, and improve. Quality is non-negotiable — a rocket that fails doesn't just lose money, it sets back an entire programme. And cost is the unlock that makes private space commercially viable, not just a government-funded moonshot.

But here's what I find most exciting: if the manufacturing process works at scale, it doesn't just make Relativity competitive — it changes the economics of experimentation in aerospace. Faster manufacturing means more launches. More launches means more data. More data means faster learning. That feedback loop, if Relativity can sustain it, is how you accelerate humanity's path to becoming a multi-planetary species. No small thing.

"The factory is the product."

— Tim Ellis, Co-founder, Relativity Space
Most aerospace companies compete on rocket design. Relativity is competing on the manufacturing process itself. That's a fundamentally different — and potentially more durable — moat.

The concerns worth taking seriously

Relativity hasn't launched a flight yet. Their first commercial launch — Terran 1 — is the first real test of whether this all works outside a factory floor and into the brutal environment of actual spaceflight. That's a significant unknown, and no amount of impressive manufacturing innovation changes the fact that a rocket that doesn't fly is just an expensive sculpture.

The competitive landscape is also unforgiving. On one side: capital-rich behemoths like Blue Origin (Jeff Bezos) and Virgin Orbit (Richard Branson), who can absorb losses for years. On the other: scrappy operators with proven launch records, like RocketLab, which had nine successful launches before Relativity had one. Relativity sits awkwardly in the middle — not rich enough to outlast the giants, not proven enough to out-credible the specialists.

To win, Relativity needs to demonstrate success quickly and consistently. In aerospace, trust is built in launches, not press releases. The technology is compelling. The business case depends on whether the rocket flies.

What makes Relativity worth watching isn't just the rocket. It's the bet they're making — that the future of aerospace is software-defined manufacturing, not hand-assembled hardware. If they're right, the implications go far beyond one company. They'll have shown the industry a new blueprint. And that's exactly what game changers do.
