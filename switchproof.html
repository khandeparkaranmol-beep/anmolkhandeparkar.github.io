SwitchProof

Prove you can switch to a cheaper AI model without losing quality.

Open models are 10–100× cheaper than frontier models like Claude, and often just as good on narrow tasks — but teams can’t prove the cheaper one is safe on their own traffic, so they overpay. SwitchProof measures exactly where the switch is safe, grades it against the truth (not the incumbent), and puts a dollar figure on it. Case study: 900 real support tickets on a public benchmark.

How It Works

Feed in real tasks. SwitchProof decides, category by category, where a cheaper model is safe — and what switching saves.

The trap everyone falls into
The obvious test is “does the cheap model agree with the one we trust?” It quietly fails. On this benchmark the two models disagreed a third of the time — and on many of those, the cheap model was the one that was right. Claude kept confusing near-identical categories like “card arrival” and “card delivery estimate.”
The fix: route on accuracy
So SwitchProof grades both models against the human answer and moves a category to the cheap model only when it’s at least as accurate there. Agreeing with your current model is not the same as being correct — and once you measure correctness, the “risky” switch becomes the better one.

The Result

900 real Banking77 support tickets, 77 categories, Claude Sonnet vs a Llama model that costs ~100× less. Every number measured on live model calls.

Naive switch — move everything to the cheap model
✗ Accuracy falls to 62% — the small model isn’t as good everywhere
✗ Confusable categories silently regress
✗ You discover it in production
SwitchProof — route only where it holds up
✓ 46% of traffic safely moves to the ~100× cheaper, 6× faster model
✓ Accuracy rises to 79% — above the 76% all-frontier baseline
✓ ~$15K/year saved at a million tickets a month
✓ The other 54% flagged to keep on the frontier model

An early prototype tested on a public benchmark, not a finished product — but every figure is real, measured on live calls, and reported with confidence intervals. The point was never the savings; it’s the guardrail that stops a cost cut from silently breaking what already works.
