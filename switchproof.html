SwitchProof

Prove you can switch to a cheaper AI model without losing quality.

Open models are 10–100× cheaper than frontier models like Claude, and often just as good on narrow tasks — but teams can’t prove the cheaper one is safe on their own traffic, so they overpay. SwitchProof measures exactly where the switch is safe, grades it against the truth (not the incumbent), and puts a dollar figure on it.

The trap most switches fall into

The obvious way to test a cheaper model is to see how often it agrees with the one you trust. On 900 real tickets the two models disagreed a third of the time. Here is a disagreement, judged against the human-labeled correct answer:

Customer ticket · Banking77
“My card has not arrived yet.”
Claude (frontier) ✗
card_delivery_estimate
Llama (100× cheaper) ✓
card_arrival
Human answer
card_arrival

Judged by agreement with Claude, the cheap model “failed” here. Judged against the human answer, it was the only one that got it right — Claude confused two near-identical categories. That is the whole idea: your current model isn’t ground truth. Agreeing with it is not the same as being correct. Across the benchmark, this happened on 16 categories where the cheap model was actually the more accurate one.

How It Works

So SwitchProof stops grading models against each other and grades them against the truth — category by category.

The Result

900 real Banking77 tickets, Claude Sonnet vs a Llama model that costs ~100× less. Routing on correctness beats both a naive switch and staying put.

Naive switch — everything to the cheap model
✗ Accuracy falls to 62% — the small model isn’t as good everywhere
✗ Confusable categories silently regress
✗ You discover it in production
SwitchProof — route only where it holds up
✓ 46% of traffic moves to the ~100× cheaper, 6× faster model
✓ Accuracy rises to 79% — above the 76% all-frontier baseline
✓ The other 54% flagged to keep on the frontier model

An early prototype tested on a public benchmark, not a finished product — but every figure is real, measured on live model calls, and reported with confidence intervals. The point was never the savings; it’s the guardrail that stops a cost cut from silently breaking what already works.
